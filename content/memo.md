# Monument memo

The partnership is being asked to commit $20M to a 50-year asset. I spent time with the deck, the pro forma, and the meeting notes, and built a parametric engine to test the scenarios more rigorously than I could otherwise. This memo summarizes what I found and what I think is worth the partnership talking through together.

Before the analysis: the team is strong, the master plan is materially better than Yellowstone Club or Wasatch Peaks Ranch at the same scale, the Foundation as a Private Operating Foundation is creative tax and stewardship engineering, and the Powder Haven adjacency is a real structural advantage. The team has been transparent with us throughout, which is what made this analysis possible.

What follows is offered in that spirit. The intent is to support the partnership's diligence, not redirect what the team has built.

---

## 1. The frame

What is this deal supposed to do over 50 years? The deck focuses on a different time horizon. The pro forma asks a 6-to-10 year hold question, which is the right question for a real estate investment but only part of what Monument is.

The deal carries three horizons. New equity at 6-to-10 years. Foundation in perpetuity. Land at 50 years or longer, with wildlife corridors and watersheds on still longer scales. These horizons are not in conflict. They are choices about which one the deal optimizes for. Section 3 walks through four scenarios that make that choice explicit.

The land is not generic real estate either. 15,000 acres in the northern Utah headwaters sits at the top of the Weber and Bear drainages, hosts a SNOTEL site installed in 2022 because the hydrology warrants continuous measurement, holds wintering range for an elk herd at population objective, and adjoins an IDA-designated park. What happens on the property affects systems beyond it. Snowpack drains downhill. Elk move across fence lines. Light spills upward. The 15,000 acres is where the deal starts, not where it stops.

The team's plan optimizes well for the 6-to-10 year horizon. The analysis below explores how the numbers shift when the longer horizons are weighted alongside it.

## 2. Open questions

Eight items the analysis surfaced that I think are worth the partnership's attention. Each is sourced. The team's perspective is acknowledged where I have it from the deck, meeting notes, or our conversations.

### 2.1 The cash flow models 27 lots while the master plan shows 50

The master plan resolves to 50 lots across the Monument properties. The cash flow monetizes 27. Of the 42 ranch rows populated in the pro forma, 15 carry $0 prices marked Firm; 8 placeholder rows also carry $0. That leaves 27 revenue-producing lots at $146M total. Foundation contribution from those 27 lots is $32.4M.

The most likely reading is that the 15 Firm lots were monetized in a prior round and the 8 placeholders are forward inventory not yet priced. That would explain the numbers cleanly. Even so, the relationship between the 27-lot cash flow and the 50-lot narrative is worth understanding more fully. If the unaccounted lots are forward inventory, total deal revenue at scale would be roughly double what the cash flow shows. If they are already monetized in prior rounds, the layered economics matter to anyone underwriting the new equity. The team can probably resolve this in a paragraph.

### 2.2 The land's capacity is close to its limits

Snowpack: peak Utah snowpack declined 16 percent since 1979 per USU Janet Quinney Lawson Institute. Powder Mountain's SNOTEL site was installed at the Weber-Bear headwaters in 2022 because the hydrology warrants continuous measurement.

Wildlife: per UDWR April 2026 recommendations, statewide mule deer sits at 73 percent of objective; elk at or just above. In June 2024 the wildlife board approved additional antlerless permits on Ensign Ranches and four other northern Utah CWMUs because elk populations were causing long-term habitat damage from overgrazing.

Roads: TRCP and Colorado Parks and Wildlife threshold is one linear mile per square mile of habitat. Monument at 23.4 square miles must stay under approximately 23 miles. Back-of-envelope from the deck and meeting notes puts Monument at 35-55 miles, 1.5 to 2.4 times the threshold.

Light: cumulative static lumens at full build-out, estimated from the deck's structure list at Weber County code maximums, sits at 170,000 to 320,000. A small rural Utah main street runs 50,000 to 100,000.

Monument's 42 ranch lots on 15,000 acres is dramatically lighter than Yellowstone Club's 750 households on 15,200 or Wasatch Peaks Ranch's planned 750 on 12,700. The team has built a footprint that compares well to the regional peer set. The question worth thinking through is whether the headroom that comparison implies actually exists, given that the constraints are absolute rather than relative to peers, and that Monument is the largest planned disturbance the headwaters has faced. A capacity-constrained sensitivity case in the model would help the partnership size what risk-adjusted volume the property can actually carry.

### 2.3 Cache County zoning

Cache County Code Title 17.10.030.B states for FR-40 zoning: "Year round residences are prohibited. The maximum occupancy period of any dwelling shall be limited to one hundred eighty (180) days per year."

The path forward is a rezone, and that path is well-precedented in the immediate area. Powder Mountain rezoned 1,621 of its Cache County acres on February 25, 2025, by a 4-3 Council vote.

Wasatch Peaks Ranch offers context worth holding in mind. Five Morgan County residents petitioned to put the 2019 zoning change to public vote under Utah's constitutional referendum right. The county clerk rejected the petition. Four years of litigation followed. In December 2023 Judge Noel Hyde issued a preliminary injunction halting all construction. WPR settled in January 2024 with a 2,300-acre conservation easement and an undisclosed funding commitment to Morgan County. They had raised $92M and built ski lifts during the litigation.

Cache and Weber are different jurisdictions than Morgan, and Brent Bateman at Dentons is experienced at this work. The team has reason for confidence on the legal path. What's worth talking through is the timeline cushion if the political layer takes longer than the base case assumes. The dark-sky community in the Ogden Valley is organized enough to be a real factor in that timing.

### 2.4 The Foundation's sizing question

Projected Foundation operating expenses by 2032 are $2.4 to $2.6M annually per the deck and meeting notes. Investment yield at 5 to 7 percent on a $30 to $40M principal sits at $1.5 to $2.8M. The shortfall during 2027 to 2031 is closed by initiation fees and per-lot Foundation contributions at $1.2M per Phase 1 lot. After Year 6, when sales stop, the fund depends on grants and donations to cover the operating gap.

The pro forma's grant schedule reflects this: zero through September 2027, then $250K per year through mid-2028, then $500K per year steady state. Total grants over the model horizon are $4,937.5K. The Foundation reaches $33.5M at horizon, almost entirely from the $32.4M of lot contributions. Yield is more than consumed by expenses. The Foundation does not grow meaningfully beyond what the lot contributions put into it.

The team's design assumes the Foundation becomes self-sustaining at maturity through compound yield plus ongoing donations. That's a reasonable design. Will Munger's USU, UDWR, and NRCS partnerships are fundraising-capable. The thing to think through is that three assumptions have to hold simultaneously for the design to work: donations at the projected rate, yield at the projected level, expenses at the projected level. Yield from 7 to 5 percent drops principal by roughly 20 percent at horizon. Grants are not contractually committed.

A deeper question worth surfacing is the relationship between Foundation sizing and Foundation ambition. The deck's framing of the Foundation as the engine of regional stewardship implies an ambition that real ecological work at that scale typically requires $80 to $100M of committed capital to fund. The Foundation as currently sized is closer to a property-line stewardship vehicle. Aligning sizing with the level of ambition the team wants to project would strengthen both the Foundation's mission and the deal's narrative coherence.

### 2.5 New equity before tax determinations

The Monument Ranch Foundation has been established and IRS Form 1023 filed. The POF determination is pending. The investor pitch promises $3 of tax benefit per $1 invested. That ratio depends on the Foundation qualifying as a POF, the donation surviving the 2.5x rule under IRC §170(h)(7), the HBU appraisal surviving the "legally permissible" prong of Tax Court precedent, and donative intent surviving scrutiny.

The 2.5x rule. If a §170(h) qualified conservation contribution by a partnership exceeds 2.5 times the sum of each member's relevant basis, the deduction is statutorily disallowed. The team's path is to structure as a non-§170(h) charitable contribution to a POF, which avoids the 2.5x rule. That structure exists and is defensible. It is also novel, which puts the entire deduction in the donative-intent and POF-qualification analysis.

Four recent Tax Court decisions (Beaverdam Creek Holdings, T.C. Memo 2025-53; Ranch Springs, 164 T.C. No. 6, 2025; Jackson Stone South, T.C. Memo 2025-96; Excelsior Aggregates, T.C. Memo 2024-60) rejected DCF-based yield-plan valuations as "fantasy economics." In Beaverdam Creek the court accepted the HBU but rejected the methodology, reducing a $22M deduction to under $200K. The deck's HBU valuation uses DCF analysis on gross retail revenue minus infrastructure CapEx applied to 300 hypothetical 40-acre lots under FR-40 zoning, which is the methodology the courts have been rejecting. The "legally permissible" prong is also testable given that FR-40 prohibits year-round residence.

OBBBA 2026, effective January 1, 2026: 35 percent cap on top-bracket itemized deductions versus the 37 percent marginal rate, plus a 0.5 percent AGI floor on charitable deductions. Combined effect is roughly a 10 percent reduction in tax benefit versus 2025 rules. The deck's $3:$1 leverage claim is closer to $2.70 to $2.80 after OBBBA, before audit risk.

The structural design has real merit and tier-one counsel has built it carefully. What's worth thinking through is timing: investor capital is contemplated before the determinations are confirmed. Staging commitment around milestones, with investor capital held in escrow until the IRS POF determination clears and tax counsel opinion is delivered, is one path to manage this without changing the underlying structure. The Staged scenario in Section 3 illustrates what that path looks like in the numbers.

### 2.6 Four pillars at full scale

Four pillars coexist easily at small scale. At full scale they compete for the same hours on the same acreage.

The activity calendar is the math. Heli skiing and cat skiing operate November through April. The snowmobile network covers the same season. UTV powder routes overlap. Hunting season runs September through November. Ranching is year-round.

Cassirer et al. 1992 found that elk in low-disturbance areas flee 1,675 meters from a skier; elk habituated to human activity flee only 40 meters. Habituation happens, and the question becomes what kind of wildlife experience members are selling each other after the elk have habituated. Gill et al. 2025, in the Journal of Wildlife Management, documented caribou home-range expansion when heli-skiing was reduced during COVID, direct evidence that mechanized recreation restricts ungulate movement at the scales Monument proposes.

Members visit 2 to 4 times per year, not continuously, and marquee mechanized activities operate 60 to 90 days at peak. That gives the pillars meaningful room to coexist. The question worth thinking through is which pillar takes the marginal hour at full Monument scale, with 42 ranches plus 50 social plus public CWMU hunters plus member guests, October through April. The Refuge marketing claim works at smaller scale or with lighter mechanized scope; at full scale and full scope, the pillars start trading off against each other. The scenarios in Section 3 make that trade-off explicit at each scale.

### 2.7 Water costs

Water costs are real and are not itemized in the pro forma.

Monument's parcels straddle two water rights administrative areas: Weber/Ogden River (Area 35) on the south, Bear/Cache Valley (Area 25) on the north. The Ogden Valley sits under the State Engineer's interim Ground-water Management Plan. The Weber Basin Water Conservancy District operates a Replacement Water program: anyone seeking to develop a new well in the Weber Basin must contract with WBWCD to release storage rights downstream to replace the groundwater they extract.

Per-lot water needs include the building envelope plus livestock water plus irrigation. Multiplied across 42 family ranches plus three lodge nodes (the LZ with multiple lodging structures, the Corrals with ranch house and guest cabins, the Shed) plus livestock water for 4,000 sheep, the aggregate demand is substantial. Each component needs water rights or a replacement-water contract or both.

Don Barnett at Kirton McConkie is competent water counsel, and the Replacement Water program exists specifically to enable new development. Properties throughout the Ogden Valley have navigated this regulatory layer for decades, so the path is well-traveled. What would help the partnership is having water cost itemized in the cash flow as a line item. If 8 to 12 lots fall out due to water constraints or replacement-water cost, the $146M revenue compresses to roughly $80 to $105M. A water-constrained sensitivity case in the model would size that exposure.

### 2.8 Equity sizing

The plan is sized as a $20M new equity commitment. The Project Summary Sources and Uses shows peak equity deployment of $10M. The Peak Funding sub-model shows a construction-period cash trough at approximately $8.9M in 2027. The trough sits inside the $10M revolver alone.

The construction does not appear to require the new equity to fund it. The revolver covers the trough by roughly $1M. That leaves approximately $11M of the $20M committed not directly financing construction.

The candidate uses are legitimate and well-understood: timing risk coverage, GP true-up obligations, distribution flexibility, execution-risk buffer, and operating-model gap if sales slip. The partnership would benefit from an itemized breakdown of how the $11M maps across these uses. That itemization would let partners think through which uses they want to fund at what size, rather than committing in aggregate.

A separate observation worth flagging: the GP fee stream is calculated on committed capital rather than deployed capital. The larger the raise, the larger the fee stream, regardless of deployment. This is a structural feature partners may want to factor into their thinking on raise sizing.

On the 2031 debt balloon: the Peak Funding cash flow shows accumulated sales proceeds retire the balloon with about $8.7M of cushion. The new equity does not appear required for debt retirement under the Base case. If the partnership's understanding has been that the equity helps retire the legacy debt, this is worth reconciling with the cash flow.

## 3. What the analysis shows

I built a parametric engine to run the four scenarios cleanly. The team's pro forma is a per-lot model: every lot has its own detail row with a hard-coded sale year and price. Lot count is not an input. It is the number of populated rows. The team's scenario columns flex per-lot intensity (price, contribution, scope) while holding lot count constant. The rebuilt engine has lot count as a real input flowing through revenue, development cost, Foundation contribution, and debt.

The engine reconciles to the team's six headline numbers at Base Case: total revenue $146M, levered XIRR 16.2 percent, MOIC 1.79x, net profit $7.9M, Foundation end balance $33.5M, total pre-existing debt service $30.8M. Two headlines carry a documented +0.6 percent residual traced to PMT amortization against the team's specific debt schedule. The other four are exact or within rounding.

### 3.1 The four scenarios

Each scenario is internally coherent. Each is defensible. The partnership decides which question it wants the deal to optimize for.

| Scenario | Lots | Phase pricing ($K) | Foundation per lot ($K) | Revenue ($M) | Foundation end ($M) | Realized tax ($M) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Base | 27 | 5.0 / 5.5 / 6.0 | 1,200 | 146.0 | 33.3 | 97 |
| Staged | 27 | 5.5 / 6.0 / 6.5 | 1,400 | 159.5 | 35.4 | 91 |
| Compact | 32 | 6.5 single | 1,800 | 208.0 | 68.0 | 138 |
| Conservation | 25 | 7.5 single | 3,200 | 187.5 | 82.2 | 192 |

Base reconciles exactly to the team's pro forma. Staged sits on the same reconciled basis (same lot count, same member base) and shows what changes if rigor is priced into the offer rather than added as overlay. Compact and Conservation flex correctly on revenue, Foundation, peak funding, and tax benefit. XIRR and MOIC for these two are deferred to the Monte Carlo treatment below, because the deterministic engine's club operating cost structure has not been fully validated against smaller-member scenarios.

### 3.2 The counterintuitive tax finding

Realized tax benefit scales with per-lot Foundation contribution times realization rate times lots. Conservation at 25 lots times $3,200 per-lot times $3 leverage times 0.8 realization produces $192M of realized tax benefit. Base at 27 lots times $1,200 per-lot times $3 times 1.0 realization produces $97M. Conservation captures nearly twice Base in absolute tax dollars, despite the 0.8 haircut.

Per-lot contribution scales nearly 3x. The 0.8 realization haircut applies to a larger base. The intuition that conservation-leaning scenarios sacrifice tax benefit is exactly wrong on the absolute-dollar dimension. The tradeoff is in cash-on-cash equity return: higher per-lot Foundation contribution means less flows back to investors.

### 3.3 The Monte Carlo

I ran 10,000 iterations against each of the four scenarios using five stochastic drivers (snowpack, sales velocity, tax realization, entitlement delay, Foundation yield) with documented correlation structure. Two tiers of success criteria: survival (deal stays viable on the partnership's basic terms) and objective (deal meets the partnership's full stated objectives, including $80M Foundation at regional scale per the deck's own framing).

| Scenario | Survival rate | Full objective | Median XIRR | Median MOIC |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Base | 0.7% | 0.0% | -2.9% | 0.67x |
| Staged | 12.4% | 0.0% | 8.2% | 1.33x |
| Compact | 34.4% | 3.3% | 61.7% | 5.06x |
| Conservation | 63.4% | 47.0% | 44.7% | 3.65x |

Conservation is the strongest scenario by survival rate and by full-objective achievement. Even Conservation meets all six partnership objectives only about 47 percent of the time. About half the time the deal works on the partnership's stated terms. About half the time it does not. That's worth planning around.

### 3.4 What kills each scenario when it dies

The stochastic engine shows specific failure mechanisms behind the survival rates. Snowpack drives scope and IRR collapse: a weak winter compresses the buyer pool and pressures lot pricing simultaneously. Foundation yield drives Foundation-target failure: yields below the 7 percent assumption compound over twelve years into material principal compression. Entitlement delay drives debt-retirement failure and dues-coverage failure together: delayed sales push the 2032 balloon into refinance territory at 7.5 percent while simultaneously starving the operating model of dues income.

The four scenarios' debt-retirement failure rates: Base 9 percent, Staged 7 percent, Compact 2 percent, Conservation 14 percent. Conservation, the scenario the analysis points toward, has the highest debt-retirement failure rate because its smaller revenue base has less margin against the fixed pre-existing debt service. Worth knowing.

### 3.5 Structural protections that improve any chosen scenario

Two refinements would move any chosen scenario toward better odds. First, closing-condition entitlement protection: investor capital held in escrow until the IRS POF determination and tax counsel opinion clear, and zoning rezone secured before lot pricing is publicly committed. This is borrowed from the Staged scenario's design but applies to any choice. Second, Foundation yield floor or larger per-lot contribution sizing: the deal underwrites top-ups if realized yield underperforms a threshold, or contribution sizes up to a level that does not depend on yield compounding to land at target.

Even with these protections, the partnership still has to choose. The analysis does not make the decision.

---

This memo presents the analysis rather than advocating for a scenario. Each of the four is internally coherent. Each is defensible. Each asks the partnership for something different.

That said, the analysis points toward Conservation First as the strongest scenario by both survival rate and full-objective achievement. Even Conservation works on the partnership's stated terms only about half the time. That's worth planning around, and it suggests the structural protections in Section 3.5 are not optional refinements but the difference between a deal that works half the time and one that works most of the time.

The four-pillar framework, the Foundation as structural innovation, the team's design improvements over the comparables, and the plan as the team has structured it all remain available. The intent of this analysis is to support the partnership in thinking through the commitment with as much information as possible.

The team's transparency throughout, in the deck, in the meeting notes, and in our conversations, made the analysis possible. That kind of openness is worth acknowledging.

The question we're deciding together is what we want this place to be in fifty years. Everything else is downstream of that.
