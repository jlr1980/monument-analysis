## 1. Great starting point

Before anything else, what the team has built is real.

### 1.1 The people

Hart Howerton on the master plan. Faure Halverson on the architecture, with Kip Halverson carrying his Yellowstone Club work into this project. Forge and Finish on development, with Spencer and Greg Lane bringing Rock Creek Montana experience. Brent Bateman at Dentons on land. Paul Jones at Hale Wood on tax, with Greenberg Traurig as co-counsel. Don Barnett at Kirton McConkie on water. Will Munger on wildlife and range, recently hired and aligned.

This is a tier-one team. The firms have done this kind of work before, in this part of the West, with this kind of buyer. We should not pretend that the team is the problem.

### 1.2 The design

The master plan is genuinely better than what Yellowstone Club shipped at the same scale a generation ago. YC put 750 households on 15,200 acres around a centralized lodge complex. Monument proposes 92 households (42 ranches plus 50 social) on 15,000 acres, dispersed across nodes (the LZ, the Corrals, the Shed) with 80 percent of the acreage held in conservation.

The dispersed-node layout matters. It distributes infrastructure across the property rather than concentrating it. It reduces road density per node. It separates the four pillars (Ski, Hunt, Ranch, Refuge) spatially in ways YC's centralized model never did. The four-pillar framework itself is coherent. It is not the usual western development pitch of amenities stacked next to lots. It is an organizing question: what do you do at Monument?

The 80 percent conservation footprint is the most ambitious claim of any comparable in this region. Wasatch Peaks Ranch's original plan held zero conservation. Yellowstone Club settled at 20 percent. Monument starts at 80. That number deserves to be acknowledged before we examine whether the operational reality can keep that promise.

### 1.3 The Foundation as structural innovation

The Monument Ranch Foundation is creative tax and stewardship engineering. Structured as a Private Operating Foundation rather than as a syndicated conservation easement, it sits in a categorically different place under tax law than the SCET structures the IRS has been dismantling. The POF must work the land. That is Will Munger's mandate, and a real one, not a fundraising hook. The deduction rests on active charitable purpose rather than passive valuation arbitrage.

This is sophisticated. Tier-one counsel structured it. The architecture, in principle, can survive the audit scrutiny the IRS has been bringing to bear on western land-and-tax deals. Section 3 turns to the timing and sizing questions. The structure itself, though, is a real piece of work, and the partnership should know that the team did not pick the easy path.

### 1.4 The Powder Haven adjacency

Reed Hastings's acquisition of Powder Mountain and the conversion to Powder Haven gives Monument something almost no comparable has: institutional underwriting of the ski amenity, on the other side of the fence, for the entire economic life of this asset. PowMow is not going away as a private club. The right-of-way easement on existing PowMow roads is non-trivial. The demand-side wealth concentration that Powder Haven has assembled is precisely the buyer pool Monument will draw from.

This is a structural advantage and it should be named as such.

### 1.5 The honest acknowledgment

In the April 30 quarterly meeting notes, the team wrote this:

> It is acknowledged that not all of these lots may survive the final planning due to potential issues like water constraints or conflicts with hunting and wildlife concerns.

That sentence does work for the partnership that the deck itself does not. It tells us the team has internalized the substrate question even if the cash flow has not yet been updated to reflect it. It tells us we are working with people who can hear the question being asked in this memo without being defensive about it. It gives us a shared starting point the partnership can stand on together.

The memo proceeds in that spirit. The deal works. The team is real. The Foundation is creative. The design is genuinely better than the comparables. Section 3 names the gaps, and every gap in Section 3 is one the team itself has either already acknowledged or has the standing to address.

## 2. The frame

What is this deal supposed to do over 50 years?

That is a question the deck does not ask. The pro forma asks a different question: what return does the new equity make on a 6-to-10 year hold? It is a reasonable question for a real estate deal. It is not the right question for what Monument actually is.

### 2.1 Different horizons, different deals

The deal has at least three time horizons baked into it. The new equity is sized to a 6-to-10 year hold, the standard horizon for real estate investment. The Foundation is sized for an indefinite life, with principal held in perpetuity and yield drawing down operating expenses. The land itself is a 50-year asset, or a 100-year asset, or longer. The wildlife corridor, the snowpack at Powder Mountain headwaters, the wintering range below the ridge: those exist on time scales that have nothing to do with quarterly return targets.

These horizons are not in conflict. They are choices about which horizon the deal is built to serve. The new equity asks the partnership to commit at the 6-to-10 year horizon. The Foundation asks the partnership to commit at the multigenerational horizon. The four scenarios in Section 5 are four different answers to which of those horizons the deal optimizes for.

This is not a critique of the deck. It is a reframing of what the partnership is actually choosing.

### 2.2 The property is substrate, not real estate

15,000 acres in the northern Utah headwaters is not a generic asset. The land sits at the top of the Weber and Bear river drainages. Powder Mountain hosts one of Utah's 137 SNOTEL sites, installed at the headwaters in 2022 because the hydrology warrants continuous measurement. The acreage is wintering range for an elk herd the state manages at population objective. The adjacent neighbor, North Fork Park, holds an International Dark Sky designation, one of the only IDA parks within two hours of a major urban center.

What this means is that whatever happens on this property cascades. Snowpack drains downhill. Elk move across fence lines. Light spills upward into the sky over neighbors who have spent more than twenty-five years protecting the dark. The 15,000 acres is not where the deal stops; it is where the deal starts. The substrate question is not "what do we do on the land," it is "what do we do to the larger system this land is part of."

That framing changes the math on every gap in Section 3. The Foundation is not a $30-40M financial vehicle; it is the capital that funds the answer to the substrate question. The mech rec scope is not a cost line; it is a commitment about what the wildlife corridor will tolerate. The dark-sky budget is not an ordinance compliance issue; it is a regional relationship.

### 2.3 The four pillars implicitly choose for you

The deck names four pillars: Ski, Hunt, Ranch, Refuge. They are presented as coexisting. Members can ski in the morning, hunt in the afternoon, watch the cattle move in the evening, and feel that they are in a refuge throughout.

At small scale, four pillars coexist. At 25 lots with reduced mech rec, the marketing claim and the operational reality match. At 42 ranches plus 50 social members plus public CWMU hunters plus member guests, with heli skiing from November through April and elk hunting from September through November and snowmobile trails groomed across the wintering range, the four pillars are competing for the same hours on the same acreage. The math gets tight.

The partnership is not really choosing whether to honor all four pillars. The partnership is choosing which pillar gets the marginal hour. Each scenario in Section 5 makes that choice differently. Plan As Proposed gives the marginal hour to whichever amenity is most monetizable. Conservation First gives the marginal hour to the refuge. The intermediate scenarios trade off in between.

### 2.4 The vernacular

What we are talking about is something our grandfathers would have understood. This is a working ranch, in a working valley, in a part of Utah where land has been passed down across three and four generations. We know what it looks like when a family stewards a place well, and we know what it looks like when a family extracts from it and moves on. We have stood at fence lines our great-grandfathers built. We know the difference.

The intergenerational tradeoff is not abstract. It is the question that gets asked at every Sunday dinner where the land comes up. What did we add to the place? What did we take out? What will the grandchildren be looking at when they stand here?

The memo presents the deal in those terms because that is the term in which most of us, sitting at this table, already think about land. The deck speaks the language of an investor pitch. This memo speaks the language we use among ourselves when we are deciding what kind of legacy we want.

## 3. The gaps

These are eight places where the deck and the cash flow do not yet match the deal's own stated ambitions. Each one is sourced. For each, the team's strongest counter-argument is on the record. The question is not whether the team will eventually address these. The question is whether the partnership commits new equity before they are addressed or after.

### 3.1 The cash flow models 27 lots, not 50

The deck describes 42 ranches plus 50 social members across a master plan with 50 lots. The cash flow monetizes 27.

Working through the pro forma's Real Estate Sales tab, the master plan resolves to 50 lots across the Monument 1, 2, 3, and 4 properties. Of the 42 populated ranch rows, 15 carry $0 contract prices and a "Firm" status: presented as already sold and generating no marginal revenue in the new offering. The 8 placeholder rows that complete the master plan to 50 also carry $0 prices: deferred or unpriced.

That leaves 27 revenue-producing lots: 10 in Phase 1 at $5,000K, 12 in Phase 2 at $5,500K, 5 in Phase 3 at $6,000K. Total $146M at $5,407K average. Foundation contribution from those 27 lots is exactly 27 times $1.2M, or $32.4M.

The most generous reading is that the 15 Firm lots were monetized in a prior round and the 8 placeholders are forward inventory the team has not yet priced. That reading reconciles the discrepancy without changing the cash flow as presented.

Where the gap survives: the deck's $150M aggregate revenue narrative implicitly assumes all 50 lots will eventually contribute at scenario prices. The cash flow only contemplates 27. If the 23 unaccounted lots are forward inventory not yet priced, the deck overstates total deal revenue. If they are already monetized in prior rounds, the layered economics matter to anyone evaluating the new equity. Either way, the new equity is being asked to commit against a 27-lot cash flow while the narrative around the deal contemplates almost twice that.

### 3.2 Substrate carrying capacity is close to, in places past, the threshold

The substrate question is whether 15,000 acres at the proposed scale can carry the proposed activity. The evidence says it is close, in places past, the threshold.

Snowpack. Per the Utah State University Janet Quinney Lawson Institute's annual report to the Governor and Legislature, peak Utah snowpack has declined 16 percent since 1979. The trend is gradual but measured. Powder Mountain hosts one of Utah's 137 SNOTEL sites, installed at the Weber-Bear headwaters in 2022 because the hydrology warrants continuous measurement. The ski amenity carries embedded climate risk that the deck does not stress-test.

Wildlife. Per UDWR April 2026 permit recommendations, Utah's statewide mule deer population is at 73 percent of management objective. Elk are at or just above objective. In June 2024 the wildlife board approved additional antlerless permits on Ensign Ranches and four other northern Utah CWMUs because elk populations were causing "long-term habitat damage from overgrazing... impacts to the struggling deer populations in the area." The deck markets scientific management. In northern Utah CWMUs in 2024, scientific management currently means culling elk to reduce habitat damage.

Roads and habitat security. The TRCP and Colorado Parks and Wildlife 2022 threshold is one linear mile of route per square mile of habitat. Monument's 23.4 square miles means total roads, improved trails, cat routes, and snowmobile network must stay under approximately 23 miles to remain inside that threshold. A defensible back-of-envelope estimate, built from the deck and meeting notes, places Monument at 35-55 miles of vehicle and improved-trail infrastructure: 1.5 to 2.4 miles per square mile. At or above the threshold.

Light. Cumulative static lumens at full build-out, estimated from the deck's structure list and Weber County's code-compliant per-fixture maximums, sits at 170,000 to 320,000 lumens. A small rural commercial main street runs 50,000 to 100,000. Monument's static lighting budget is several times larger than a small Utah town's, distributed across a property whose adjacent neighbor holds an IDA designation predicated on regional darkness.

The team's strongest counter: substrate concerns are general environmental concerns that apply to every western development. Monument's design responds with 80 percent conservation, dispersed nodes, professional range and wildlife management. Compared to Yellowstone Club's 750 households on 15,200 acres or Wasatch Peaks Ranch's 750 on 12,700, Monument's footprint is dramatically lighter.

Where the gap survives: the team is not the cause of the substrate question, but Monument is the largest planned disturbance the substrate has faced in this drainage. The team's own April 30 admission about water constraints and wildlife conflicts is the strongest single piece of evidence that the substrate is bumping against limits at the proposed scale. The substrate is telling the team what it can carry. The cash flow has not yet been updated to reflect the answer.

### 3.3 Cache County zoning prohibits what Monument intends to sell

Cache County Code, Title 17.10.030.B (cited in the 2013 ordinance amendment Ord. 2013-06), states for FR-40 (Forest Recreation 40) zoning: "Year Round Occupancy Restriction: Year round residences are prohibited. The maximum occupancy period of any dwelling shall be limited to one hundred eighty (180) days per year."

If Monument intends to sell year-round family ranches, current FR-40 zoning does not permit them. The team's path forward is a rezone to Resort Recreation or equivalent. Powder Mountain just did exactly this for 1,621 of its Cache County acres. That rezone passed Cache County Council on February 25, 2025, by a 4-3 vote. One vote either way.

The Wasatch Peaks Ranch precedent is closer to the relevant fact pattern than the deck acknowledges. Five Morgan County residents petitioned to put the 2019 WPR zoning change to public vote under Utah's constitutional referendum right. The county clerk rejected the petition. Four years of litigation followed. In December 2023 Judge Noel Hyde issued a preliminary injunction halting all WPR construction, with the line "Constitutional rights are not for sale." WPR settled in January 2024 with a 2,300-acre conservation easement plus an undisclosed funding commitment to Morgan County resident projects. They had raised at least $92M and built ski lifts during the litigation, on a vested-rights theory the court eventually rejected.

The team's strongest counter: WPR was a Morgan County procedural challenge with a specific organized plaintiff group. Cache and Weber counties are different jurisdictions with different political dynamics. Brent Bateman at Dentons is competent land counsel. Forge and Finish has Utah development experience. Monument's 80 percent conservation framing is more politically defensible than WPR's 0 percent original conservation. The 6-year sales window includes meaningful entitlement buffer.

Where the gap survives: WPR's procedural risk profile is not Morgan-specific. Once a rezone action is taken, Utah residents have referendum rights under the state constitution. The dark-sky community in the Ogden Valley is organized, articulate, and politically networked. The Ogden Valley Chapter of IDA, the Ogden Valley GEM Committee, and Weber State Astronomy together represent precisely the kind of constituency that organizes referenda. WPR's $92M and four years of litigation are sunk costs that came from assuming the political layer would resolve smoothly. The Monument pro forma assumes the same.

### 3.4 The Foundation runs structural deficit unless three assumptions hold

Per deck slide 32 and the April 30 meeting notes, projected Foundation operating expenses by 2032 are $2.4 to $2.6M annually. Investment yield at 5 to 7 percent on a $30 to $40M principal sits at $1.5 to $2.8M. The shortfall during 2027 to 2031 is closed by initiation fees and per-lot Foundation contributions flowing in at $1.2M per Phase 1 lot sold. After Year 6, when sales stop, the fund depends on grants and donations to cover the operational gap.

The pro forma's grant schedule reflects this: zero through September 2027, then $250K per year through mid-2028, then $500K per year steady state. Total grants over the model horizon are $4,937.5K. The Foundation reaches $33.5M at horizon, almost entirely from the $32.4M of lot contributions. Yield is more than consumed by expenses, and the $4.9M of projected grants provides the small remaining surplus. The Foundation does not grow meaningfully beyond what the lot contributions put into it.

The team's strongest counter: the Foundation is sized to be self-sustaining at maturity through compound investment yield plus ongoing donations and grants. Will Munger's USU, UDWR, and NRCS partnerships are actively fundraising-capable. $30M compounding at 6 to 7 percent grows to $40M plus by Year 15. The math closes once the donations are included.

Where the gap survives: three assumptions must hold simultaneously. Donations at the projected rate. Yield at the projected level. Expenses at the projected level. If any one slips, principal compresses. The Foundation end balance is materially sensitive to the yield assumption; a drop from 7 to 5 percent meaningfully compresses the principal at horizon. The grants are speculative and not contractually committed. The expense projection is the team's projection, not an independent estimate.

There is a deeper structural question hiding inside the sizing one. A $30 to $40M Foundation administers Monument's stewardship. Real ecological work at regional scale, the kind the Foundation's framing implies, runs closer to $80 to $100M of committed capital. The Foundation as currently sized is a property-line stewardship vehicle. The deck implies more. That gap between sizing and ambition is the most consequential framing question in the deal.

### 3.5 The deal contemplates new equity before the tax determinations are in hand

Per April 30 meeting notes, the Monument Ranch Foundation has been established and IRS Form 1023 has been filed. The POF determination is pending. The investor pitch promises $3 of tax benefit per $1 invested. That ratio depends on the Foundation qualifying as a POF, the donation surviving the 2.5x rule under IRC §170(h)(7), the HBU appraisal surviving the "legally permissible" prong of Tax Court precedent, and donative intent surviving scrutiny in light of the documented leverage pitch.

The 2.5x rule. If a §170(h) qualified conservation contribution by a partnership exceeds 2.5 times the sum of each member's relevant basis, the deduction is statutorily disallowed. The team's path through this is to structure as a non-§170(h) charitable contribution to a Private Operating Foundation. That structure exists. It is novel, and it puts the entire deduction in the donative-intent and POF-qualification analysis rather than in the easement framework.

The HBU yield-plan precedent. Four recent Tax Court decisions (Beaverdam Creek Holdings, T.C. Memo 2025-53; Ranch Springs, 164 T.C. No. 6, 2025; Jackson Stone South, T.C. Memo 2025-96; Excelsior Aggregates, T.C. Memo 2024-60) rejected DCF-based yield-plan valuations as "fantasy economics." In Beaverdam Creek the court accepted the HBU but rejected the methodology, reducing a $22M deduction to under $200K. Monument's deck explicitly uses DCF analysis on gross retail revenue minus infrastructure CapEx applied to 300 hypothetical 40-acre lots under FR-40 zoning. That is exactly the methodology the courts have been rejecting. The "legally permissible" prong is contestable because FR-40 prohibits year-round residence: the hypothetical lots could not legally be sold as full-occupancy at the time of donation.

OBBBA 2026. Effective January 1, 2026: 35 percent cap on top-bracket itemized deductions versus the 37 percent marginal rate, plus a 0.5 percent AGI floor on charitable deductions. Combined effect is roughly a 10 percent reduction in tax benefit versus 2025 rules. The deck's $3:$1 leverage claim is closer to $2.70 to $2.80 after OBBBA, before audit risk.

The team's strongest counter: structuring outside §170(h) avoids the 2.5x rule. Greenberg Traurig and Hale Wood are tier-one counsel. The HBU yield-plan rejected in Beaverdam Creek failed because of speculative inputs, not because the methodology is impermissible. The fee-simple structure eliminates the "before/after" valuation problem that has been central to recent SCET dismantling cases.

Where the gap survives: even if every structural question clears, OBBBA still reduces the realized benefit. More importantly, investor capital is being taken in before the determinations are in hand. The team can stage commitment around milestones, IRS POF determination as closing condition, tax counsel opinion as closing condition, escrow until milestones clear, without changing the underlying structure. That is what the Staged scenario in Section 5 does.

### 3.6 Four pillars compete at the proposed scale

Four pillars coexist easily at small scale. At full scale they compete for the same hours on the same acreage.

The activity calendar is the math. Heli skiing and cat skiing operate November through April. The snowmobile network covers the same season. UTV powder routes overlap. Hunting season runs September through November. Ranching is year-round. There is no season in which the property is undisturbed.

Cassirer et al. 1992 found that elk in low-disturbance areas flee 1,675 meters from a skier; elk habituated to human activity flee only 40 meters. Habituation happens. The question is what kind of wildlife experience members are selling each other after the elk have habituated. Gill et al. 2025, in the Journal of Wildlife Management, documented caribou home-range expansion when heli-skiing was reduced during COVID, evidence that mech rec is a documented restrictor of ungulate movement at the scales Monument proposes.

The stacking is what matters. Heli plus cat plus snowmobile plus UTV plus ski touring plus roads plus heli pad operations across the same parcel produces a cumulative footprint that exceeds the sum of its parts.

The team's strongest counter: members visit 2 to 4 times per year, not continuously. Marquee mechanized activities operate 60 to 90 days per year at peak. 15,000 acres provides buffer. The four pillars have coexisted under Whisper Ridge's prior heli/cat operations.

Where the gap survives: WR's prior operations were at far smaller activity intensity than Monument's full build-out. At 42 ranches plus 50 social plus public CWMU hunters plus member guests, the disturbance footprint becomes effectively continuous from October through April. The Refuge marketing claim and the operational reality diverge. At 25 lots and reduced mech rec scope, the gap closes. At 42 lots and full mech rec, it does not.

### 3.7 Water costs are not in the cash flow

Water costs are real and they are not itemized in the pro forma.

Monument's parcels straddle two water rights administrative areas: Weber/Ogden River (Area 35) on the south, Bear/Cache Valley (Area 25) on the north. The Ogden Valley sits under the State Engineer's interim Ground-water Management Plan. The Weber Basin Water Conservancy District operates a Replacement Water program: anyone seeking to develop a new well in the Weber Basin must contract with WBWCD to release storage rights downstream to replace the groundwater they extract.

Per-lot water needs include the building envelope plus livestock water plus irrigation. Multiplied across 42 family ranches plus three lodge nodes (the LZ with multiple lodging structures, the Corrals with ranch house and guest cabins, the Shed) plus livestock water for 4,000 sheep, the aggregate water demand is substantial. Each component needs water rights or a replacement-water contract or both. None of those costs are line items in the cash flow.

The team's strongest counter: Don Barnett at Kirton McConkie is competent water counsel. WBWCD's Replacement Water program exists specifically to enable new development. Properties throughout the Ogden Valley have navigated this regulatory layer for decades. Water needs are significant but not unprecedented.

Where the gap survives: water cost is real and not disclosed. The team's April 30 acknowledgment that "not all of these lots may survive the final planning due to potential issues like water constraints" is the strongest evidence that water is a binding constraint, not a manageable line item. If 8 to 12 lots fall out due to water, the $146M cash flow revenue compresses to roughly $80 to $105M. The model has not been re-run on that assumption.

### 3.8 Whisper Ridge's failure mode has not been fully retired

Whisper Ridge ran on this same 70,000-acre footprint and failed.

Per direct confirmation, Whisper Ridge failed economically: undercapitalization plus management. Not regulatory. Not wildlife. They ran out of money on a heli/cat operating model that never produced profit. Monument's response is structurally correct: do not run heli/cat as a profit center, run it as a member amenity funded by dues members willingly pay for access.

Lesson absorbed. The harder question is whether the new operating model has stress-tested the same dependencies that broke the old one.

Monument's break-even-with-dues model assumes 92 members at $25K and $15K annual dues. The pro forma carries $17.5M total dues over the horizon. Club Operating Expenses run $43M. The gap is closed by the Foundation interest income offset plus other club revenue plus replacement reserves. That is a long chain of dependencies, every one of which is sensitive to member count.

If sales velocity runs 30 percent slower than projected, the dues base shrinks correspondingly. If the buyer pool turns out smaller than projected, member count stays under 92 indefinitely. The small Swiss Army team operating model has limited slack. One bad winter, one major equipment failure, one PR incident with the dark-sky neighbors, and the operating margin compresses fast.

The team's strongest counter: Monument is structurally different from Whisper Ridge in both capital and management. The lesson has been absorbed.

Where the gap survives: the lesson is correct, but the new model has not been stress-tested against the same dependencies that broke the old one. The $20M new equity commitment plus a $10M revolving debt facility from Cache Valley Bank is sized to entitlement and pre-development. If sales velocity slips, the operating model is what comes under pressure, not the construction draw.

### 3.9 Most of the equity commitment is not financing construction

The partnership is being asked to commit $20M (deck and April 30 meeting notes). The construction cash flow does not call on most of it.

Two layers sit inside that $20M. The team's pro forma deploys $10M of equity at peak: the maximum drawn at any single point in the modeled cash flow (Project Summary, Sources and Uses). The construction schedule itself needs less than that. The Peak Funding sub-model built for this analysis, which places each lot's revenue at its actual close month and spreads costs monthly, puts the deepest cash trough at roughly $8.9M in the Base Case, somewhere between $7M and $11M across reasonable construction-phasing assumptions. That trough, in 2027, is the real construction funding requirement. The $10M revolving facility alone covers it.

Two pools of equity sit outside that requirement. Roughly $1.1M of the $10M peak deployment is idle against the construction trough. And roughly $10M of the $20M commitment is never deployed at peak in the team's model at all. Together, about $11M of the $20M, more than half, is doing something other than financing construction.

This is not a claim that the equity is misused. It is a claim that the partnership does not yet know what it funds.

The team's strongest counter: the team has not publicly itemized the non-construction equity, and several legitimate uses could account for it. The GP true-up pays $750K each to Phil and Seth at close (deck terms). Pre-development is already $3.9M in the team's model (Pre-Dev Detail sheet); meaningful additional pre-development beyond that figure would be unusual. A development hold of this length reasonably carries distribution-timing flexibility and an execution-risk buffer. Each of those is a normal call on committed equity. One commonly cited rationale, that the equity helps retire the pre-existing debt, does not survive the Base cash flow analysis. The cumulative cash position fully retires the debt including the 2031 balloon from sales proceeds, leaving roughly $8.7M of additional cash on hand. The non-construction equity therefore funds something other than debt retirement. The open question is which ones apply, and in what amounts, not whether the money is being used properly.

Where the gap survives: the partnership cannot judge capital efficiency without the use-of-funds detail. Treat all $20M as deployed-at-risk and the equity's true exposure is overstated. Treat only the $8.9M construction trough as equity-at-risk and the partnership's actual commitment is understated. Neither number alone is honest, and the current pro forma does not break the $20M down. The partnership should ask the team for that breakdown before it commits. The GP economics also incentivize the size of the raise (close-of-deal payments and ongoing percentage fees), which means the partnership's interest in capital efficiency and the GP's interest in raise size may not fully align. The use-of-funds disclosure is what lets the partnership evaluate that alignment.

## 4. Parameter ranges

The deal is not binary. Each gap in Section 3 has a parameter that can be moved. Each parameter set produces a different financial outcome and a different risk profile. The model behind this memo makes those parameters explicit so the partnership can see, rather than imagine, what each combination produces.

### 4.1 The engine

This memo's analysis runs on a parametric engine built for the purpose. It is a separate workbook, not a marked-up copy of the team's pro forma, with nine sheets in calculation order: Inputs, Lot Schedule, Revenue, Development Cost, Foundation, Debt, Cash Flow, Peak Funding, and Reconciliation. Each sheet reads the ones before it; nothing reads backward.

The engine's discipline is reconciliation. With the scenario dropdown set to Base Case, it reproduces the team's six headline numbers: total real estate revenue, levered XIRR, MOIC, net profit, Foundation end balance, and pre-existing debt service. Four reconcile exactly or to a fraction of a percent. Net profit and MOIC carry a small documented residual, single-sourced to the pre-existing debt calculation. The Reconciliation sheet shows the comparison line by line. That is the audit trail: the engine earns the right to be trusted on the other scenarios by first reproducing the team's own.

A dropdown on the Inputs sheet selects among five scenarios: Base, Staged, Compact, Conservation, and a Custom column the partnership can edit directly. Switching the dropdown recalculates the whole model.

What flexes with the scenario: lot count, lot pricing, Foundation contribution and terms, development scope, amenity scope, and cabin construction cost. The team's original pro forma could not flex lot count at all; it was hard-coded into per-lot detail rows, which is why Compact and Conservation could not be modeled honestly against it. The rebuilt engine treats lot count as a true input that flows through revenue, development cost, Foundation contribution, and debt. That was the central reason for the rebuild.

What does not yet flex: club operating costs are held at the Base program across all scenarios, and the cabin lever is a coarse cost multiplier rather than a detailed cabin-program model. Because club operating costs and a few smaller items do not flex, the non-Base levered XIRR and MOIC are not yet validated to the standard of the Base case. Staged is the exception: it shares Base's lot count and member base, so its returns rest on the same reconciled basis. For Compact and Conservation, Section 5 reports the metrics that flex correctly and treats the precise return as a Monte Carlo question.

The Inputs sheet also carries a Stochastic Drivers block, a small set of inputs held neutral in all four named scenarios. That block is the correlation surface for the eventual Monte Carlo work. The engine as it stands is a parametric scenario model, not a stress-test framework. Scenario-level sensitivity analysis, how each output moves when a driver is varied across its plausible range, is deferred to the Monte Carlo stage, where correlated input distributions can be run properly.

### 4.2 The tax-benefit dimension is non-monotonic

Realized tax benefit in each scenario is the total Foundation contribution, times the $3 headline leverage, times the realization rate. The realization rate is a haircut for OBBBA, audit risk, and appraisal challenge: 1.0 in Base, 0.8 in the other three.

Across the four scenarios:

- Base: $1.2M per lot times 27 lots times $3 times 1.0 realization, $97M.
- Staged: $1.4M per lot times 27 lots times $3 times 0.8 realization, $90.7M.
- Compact: $1.8M per lot times 32 lots times $3 times 0.8 realization, $138M.
- Conservation: $3.2M per lot times 25 lots times $3 times 0.8 realization, $192M.

The result is not a simple ranking. Tax benefit scales with the total Foundation contribution, which is per-lot contribution times lot count. The realization haircut then either flips that result or it does not, depending on how far the per-lot contribution rose.

Staged shows the haircut winning. Its per-lot contribution rises only modestly over Base, $1.2M to $1.4M, and it carries the 0.8 haircut. The modest increase does not overcome the haircut, so Staged's realized tax benefit, $90.7M, lands slightly below Base's $97M. More Foundation contribution produced less realized tax benefit.

Compact and Conservation show the contribution scaling winning. Their per-lot contributions rise sharply, to $1.8M and $3.2M. Even under the same 0.8 haircut Staged carries, the larger total contribution dominates: Compact at $138M and Conservation at $192M both clear Base by a wide margin. Conservation, at nearly twice Base in absolute tax dollars, captures the most.

So the intuition that the conservation-leaning scenarios sacrifice tax benefit is wrong in absolute dollars: Conservation captures the most. But the dimension is non-monotonic. More Foundation contribution does not always mean more realized tax benefit, because the realization haircut can outweigh a modest contribution increase. Staged is the case that proves it.

The trade-off against the tax dimension is the cash-on-cash return. A higher per-lot Foundation contribution routes money to the Foundation rather than to investors. Conservation captures the most tax benefit and builds the largest Foundation, and it returns the least cash on the longest hold. Base does the opposite. Section 5 makes that trade-off explicit for each scenario.

## 5. Viable alternatives

The four named scenarios are not ranked by best. They are answers to four different questions about what the partnership wants the deal to optimize for. Each is internally coherent. Each has its strongest case. The memo presents them neutrally. The Summary sheet in the model carries the same content in tabular form, with live snapshots of each scenario's quantitative outputs.

### 5.1 Plan As Proposed (Base Case)

Optimize for: maximum financial return on this real estate investment, including the full tax shelter, on a 6-to-10 year hold.

The team's numbers as presented. The cash flow monetizes 27 lots at Phase 1 $5,000K, Phase 2 $5,500K, and Phase 3 $6,000K, for $146M total revenue. Foundation contribution from those lots is $32.4M, structured as $1.2M per lot. Foundation end balance at horizon, 2037, is $33.5M. Levered XIRR is 16.2 percent. MOIC is 1.79. Net profit is $7.9M on $10M of peak levered equity. Realized tax benefit at the full $3 headline times 1.0 realization rate is $97M.

Risks the new equity bears under this scenario, as developed in Section 3: IRS POF determination still pending at investor commitment; HBU yield-plan vulnerability under the four 2025 Tax Court precedents; OBBBA reducing the effective tax benefit roughly 10 percent; FR-40 entitlement gap not yet resolved; substrate strain at the proposed activity scale; Foundation sized at $30 to $40M when the framing implies $80M-plus; the lot pricing anchor, the single input the deal is most sensitive to.

What this scenario asks of the partnership: $20M new equity at existing terms, with 80 percent common dilution. The team's existing structure, timeline, and operational plan.

### 5.2 Plan With Rigor (Staged)

Optimize for: committing the equity only after the procedural risks are resolved, and pricing the rigor into the buyer offer so the cost of the wait is paid for rather than absorbed.

The first version of this scenario treated rigor as an overlay on the Base deal: Base lot prices, a larger Foundation carve, structural protections layered on top. That version did not work. The larger Foundation contribution, taken out of unchanged revenue, eroded the developer's margin into a negative modeled return. Rigor carried as a pure cost is not a viable scenario, and the model said so plainly.

Plan With Rigor is therefore built as a different product. It keeps Base's 27-lot count and construction program. What changes is that the structural protections are real, and the buyer pays for them:

- Phase 1, 2, and 3 lot prices at $5,500K, $6,000K, and $6,500K: a roughly 10 percent premium over Base. The buyer is paying for a deal with the procedural questions already answered.
- IRS POF determination as a closing condition before equity drawdown.
- Tax counsel opinion on the §170(h) versus ordinary-contribution structure as a closing condition.
- Investor capital held in escrow until those determinations clear.
- Zoning rezone secured before lot pricing is publicly committed.
- Foundation contribution per lot at $1.4M, between Base's $1.2M and the original Staged target of $1.6M: meaningful additional Foundation capital, sized so it does not consume the investor return.
- Tax-downside (0.8 realization rate) modeled as base, not as downside.

In the model: revenue of $159.5M, Foundation end balance $35.4M, realized tax benefit $90.7M, peak funding requirement $8.9M. Levered XIRR is 29.1 percent and MOIC 2.57x, both above Base. The 10 percent price premium more than covers the larger Foundation contribution and the rigor. Because Staged keeps Base's 27-lot count, construction program, club, and member base, these figures rest on the same reconciled engine basis as Base; they are the most reliable of the non-Base scenarios. One honest note on the tax line: at $90.7M, Staged's realized tax benefit is slightly below Base's $97M, because the 0.8 realization haircut more than offsets the higher per-lot contribution. Staged's case is the return and the de-risking, not the tax benefit.

The tradeoff against Plan As Proposed is roughly 12 months of timeline, the time to secure the determinations and the rezone. The equity does not bear procedural risk. It bears asset risk, after the procedural questions have been answered, and it earns a higher modeled return for the wait.

What this scenario asks: the same $20M new equity commitment, into escrow, with drawdown tied to milestones.

### 5.3 Reduced Footprint (Compact)

Optimize for: delivering to children and grandchildren the experience that brought the partnership to Monument, through a structure that pays for itself.

32 lots, all in a single phase. No Phase 2/3 entitlement layer. $6.5M average lot price. $1.8M Foundation contribution per lot. Foundation target $58M. Reduced mechanized recreation scope (0.6 multiplier on the heli, ski-pass, and snowcat lines). Legacy debt refinanced at 7.5 percent in 2032 if sales-funded payoff slips.

In the model: revenue of $208M, Foundation end balance $68.0M, peak funding requirement $7.8M, realized tax benefit $138M. Those metrics flex correctly with the scenario inputs. Investor returns run well above Base, but the precise levered XIRR and MOIC are not reported here: the non-Base return figures have not been validated to the standard of the Base case, which reconciles to the team's pro forma, and they will be calibrated at the Monte Carlo stage. What the model captures cleanly is the shape, a larger revenue base against a lighter, single-phase development program, and the directional uplift over Base is large.

What is harder to model but easier to see in the field is the substrate alignment. 32 lots single-phase is one rezone, one political surface, one entitlement window. The reduced mech rec scope signals to neighbors and to the dark-sky community that Monument is not the maximum-extraction case. The four pillars coexist more easily at 32 lots than at 42.

Risks accepted: the lot pricing assumption still binds, and a material shortfall against the premium Phase 1 price is the principal downside. The refinanced legacy debt at 7.5 percent moves cash flow but does not break the deal. Foundation sufficiency is materially better than Plan As Proposed but still short of the regional-scale framing.

What this scenario asks: same $20M new equity, single-phase commitment, faster path to entitlement clarity, reduced operating footprint.

### 5.4 Conservation First (Conservation)

Optimize for: great-grandchildren hunting the same elk herd. Monument as a multigenerational ecological asset rather than a multigenerational real estate asset. Willingness to trade meaningful financial return today for that.

25 lots at $7.5M average premium pricing. $3.2M Foundation contribution per lot. Foundation target $80M, with structural surplus. Foundation yield modeled conservatively at 5 percent. Regional contribution mandate built into Foundation operations: $1M per year for USU partnerships, watershed work, dark-sky preservation, civic engagement on regional development pressures (the Hansel Valley Stratos data center being one such pressure). Mech rec scope light. Foundation expense multiplier 0.8 to fund the regional mandate. Legacy debt refinanced at 7.5 percent in 2032 if needed.

In the model: revenue of $187.5M, Foundation end balance $82.2M, peak funding requirement $6.6M, realized tax benefit $192M. The Foundation lands just above its $80M design target, built from the $80M of lot contributions (25 lots at $3.2M) plus modest investment growth. The realized tax benefit at $192M is nearly twice Plan As Proposed's $97M in absolute dollars, per Section 4.2. As in Reduced Footprint, the precise levered XIRR and MOIC are not reported here: the non-Base return figures are not yet validated to the Base case standard and will be calibrated at the Monte Carlo stage. The directional uplift over Base is real.

The deck describes Monument's intended buyer as someone for whom "modern UHNW wealth focuses on transmitting values, not just assets." That is the deck arguing for Conservation First and not naming the scenario.

Risks accepted: smaller cash-on-cash equity return. Longer hold horizon, 10-plus years rather than 6-to-10. Higher dues and initiation fees on members. A regional commitment the partnership has to honor in practice, not just on a slide. Lot pricing risk still binds, as in every scenario.

What this scenario asks: same $20M new equity, longer hold, higher dues, higher IFs, regional contribution commitment.

### 5.5 Custom

The Scenarios sheet has a fifth column the partnership can edit directly. Custom defaults to Base Case values. The yellow cells indicate inputs that can be changed. The dropdown sets the active scenario to Custom, and the model recalculates fully downstream.

Useful for: stress-testing a single variable, building hybrid positions, exploring what happens if Foundation contribution lands between Base and Plan With Rigor, or if mech rec scope drops without changing lot count. The Custom column is the partnership's working draft of any agreement that does not match one of the four named scenarios exactly.

### 5.6 The pattern across scenarios

Reading the four scenarios as a set rather than as alternatives surfaces a structural fact about the deal:

The team's strongest defense, for every gap in Section 3, gets meaningfully stronger at smaller scale and meaningfully weaker at larger scale. The same design at 25 lots with an $80M Foundation produces a deal that survives every steel-man critique. The same design at 42 ranches with a $30M Foundation has a real risk profile in every dimension.

The gaps are not independent items. They are a system. The system tightens or loosens with scale.

Plan With Rigor sits in between. It accepts the proposed lot ambition but de-risks the structural exposures by adding closing conditions, a larger Foundation, sequenced capital deployment, and explicit downside modeling. That is the version that lets the partnership keep the team's deal mostly intact while changing the timing of when the equity bears the risk.

Reduced Footprint and Conservation First reframe the deal at smaller scale, where the substrate and the structure align more naturally.

Four scenarios. Four answers to four different questions about what the partnership wants the deal to optimize for. Each is internally coherent. Each is defensible. The partnership has to decide which question it wants the deal to serve.

## 6. Monte Carlo Findings

### 6.1 The Monte Carlo, briefly

Scenario analysis tells us how each design performs at its central case. Monte Carlo tells us how each performs across plausible variation in the inputs the deal does not control. The four scenarios in Section 5 each carry an implicit "if everything lands at expectation" assumption. The Monte Carlo asks what happens when things don't.

Mechanics. For each of the four scenarios, the same parametric engine that powers the scenario writeups was run 10,000 times. Each iteration draws values for five stochastic variables: snowpack index (normal, historical mean 0.84 against the long-run); sales velocity factor (lognormal centered at 0.9 with wider downside); tax realization rate (beta-distributed, centered around 0.85 to reflect OBBBA, audit risk, and appraisal challenge per Section 3.5); entitlement delay in months (exponential, mode at zero with a long right tail); and Foundation yield (normal, centered at the historical 6 percent mean). The first four are correlated to capture realistic joint movement: good snowpack tends to co-occur with stronger sales, entitlement delay slows sales and adds to debt-retirement pressure, and so on. Each iteration produces the same six headline numbers the scenario writeups produce, plus pass-fail flags on two tiers of structural criteria. Survival, the looser tier: 12 percent IRR floor, the scenario's own deterministic Foundation target, debt retired from sales proceeds without forcing the 7.5 percent refinance, and modeled scope sustainable on the iteration's revenue. Objective, the tighter tier: 20 percent IRR, 2.0x MOIC, an $80M Foundation balance (the regional-scale conservation threshold from Section 3.4), the same debt and scope tests, and dues coverage at steady state without Foundation-yield subsidy. A run "achieves objectives" only when all six conditions hold simultaneously.

Honest limitations. The Monte Carlo sits on the same parametric engine that produces the scenario writeups, and inherits the same caveats. Base reconciles exactly to the team's pro forma at the six headline numbers; the other three scenarios are close but not cell-for-cell exact. The Monte Carlo kernel is implemented in Python for simulation speed; on Base it reconciles exactly to the Excel engine, on Compact it diverges by +1.6 percent, on Conservation by -6.6 percent. Reported Monte Carlo distributions reflect the Python kernel's behavior; reported deterministic values reference the Excel engine. The Foundation yield distribution is centered at the historical 6 percent mean rather than the team's 7 percent assumption, which means most iterations carry a slightly lower yield than the deterministic case shows. That asymmetry is a deliberate calibration choice and is visible in the results.

### 6.2 The headline finding

| Scenario | Survival | Objective | Median XIRR | Median MOIC |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Plan As Proposed (Base) | 0.74% | 0.00% | -2.91% | 0.67x |
| Plan With Rigor (Staged) | 12.40% | 0.00% | 8.22% | 1.33x |
| Reduced Footprint (Compact) | 34.43% | 3.25% | 61.71% | 5.06x |
| Conservation First | 63.35% | 47.02% | 44.70% | 3.65x |

Read the table column by column.

Survival rate, the looser tier. Plan As Proposed clears all four survival criteria together in 74 iterations out of 10,000. Plan With Rigor, with its 10 percent price premium and tighter terms, improves to 12 percent. Reduced Footprint clears a third of iterations. Conservation First clears two thirds.

Objective achievement, the tighter tier with the $80M Foundation bar. Plan As Proposed and Plan With Rigor never achieve all six partnership objectives, in any iteration of 10,000. Reduced Footprint reaches them in 3.25 percent of runs. Conservation First reaches them in 47 percent.

Median returns. Note the gap between deterministic and Monte Carlo medians. Plan As Proposed's deterministic levered XIRR is 16.25 percent, but its median Monte Carlo XIRR is negative two percent. The Monte Carlo distribution is biased toward worse-than-deterministic outcomes for a structural reason: most iterations draw Foundation yield below the team's 7 percent (historical mean is 6 percent), most draw snowpack below the deterministic 1.0 (historical mean is 0.84), and most draw sales velocity below the deterministic 1.0 (modeled median is 0.9). The deterministic case sits at the upper edge of the input distribution rather than its center.

The 47 percent finding deserves direct treatment. Conservation First, the strongest scenario under this analysis, achieves all six partnership objectives in roughly half of all iterations. The arithmetic. Conservation's deterministic Foundation end (Section 5.4) is $82.2M, $2.2M above the $80M objective bar. The Monte Carlo distribution centers lower, around $76.7M, because the Python simulation kernel approximates revenue phasing and Foundation flows rather than mirroring the Excel engine cell-for-cell (per Section 6.1's limitations), and because the 6 percent yield assumption pulls iterations downward. Slightly more than half of iterations clear the $80M Foundation bar; the full six-criterion objective set clears in 47 percent. About half the time the deal works on the partnership's stated terms; about half the time it does not. That is the right number to plan around.

Reading the four scenarios together. Conservation First is the strongest by both criteria. Plan As Proposed essentially fails everything in this analysis: 0.74 percent survival, 0 percent objective. Plan With Rigor improves the procedural rigor but not the fundamentals; its IRR, its Foundation balance, and its MOIC all sit below the partnership objective bars in nearly every iteration. Reduced Footprint produces strong investor returns, with median XIRR of 62 percent and median MOIC above 5.0x, but its smaller Foundation does not deliver the regional-scale stewardship from Section 3.4 that the deck's framing implies.

### 6.3 What kills each scenario when it dies

The Monte Carlo tells us not only how often each scenario clears, but why each fails when it does. Three stochastic drivers do most of the failure work across the four scenarios.

**Snowpack drives scope and IRR collapse.** The correlation between iteration-level snowpack and failure on the IRR and scope criteria runs -0.69 to -0.78 across scenarios (very strong; -1.0 would mean deterministic). The mechanism: the model propagates snowpack into lot pricing, on the premise that the luxury-buyer willingness-to-pay shifts with realized powder conditions. When snowpack draws low, revenue compresses, and the scenario's modeled returns collapse. Plan With Rigor's median XIRR sits at 8.2 percent in the Monte Carlo distribution against a deterministic 29.1 percent largely because of this mechanism: most iterations draw snowpack below the deterministic 1.0, dragging XIRR down.

**Foundation yield drives Foundation-target miss.** The correlation between Foundation yield and Foundation-target failure runs -0.50 to -0.71 across scenarios. The mechanism is direct: Foundation principal accumulates from lot contributions and compounds at the realized yield over the 12-year horizon. When yield draws below the 6 percent historical mean, Foundation end falls below the scenario's target. The 47 percent Conservation finding from Section 6.2 is this mechanism playing out across 10,000 iterations: half draw enough yield to clear the $80M objective bar, half do not.

**Entitlement delay drives debt retirement and dues coverage failure together.** The correlation between entitlement delay and debt-retirement failure runs +0.55 to +0.79 across scenarios. The same correlation against dues-coverage failure runs similarly, peaking at +0.79 in Conservation. The two criteria fail in the same iterations because the same mechanism breaks both. When entitlement slips, sales close later, the peak funding need balloons (pushing the deal toward refinance instead of sales-funded retirement of the 2032 balloon), and the membership ramp lags (the club pays full operating costs while collecting partial dues). This is the Whisper Ridge failure mode from Section 3.8 quantified across the stochastic input distribution: roughly 9 percent of Plan As Proposed iterations fail debt retirement, 7 percent of Plan With Rigor, 2 percent of Reduced Footprint, and 14 percent of Conservation, with entitlement delay the dominant driver in each. Whisper Ridge is not a tail risk in this analysis; it is a meaningful slice of the modeled distribution, and it is most pronounced in the scenario the analysis points toward.

### 6.4 What the analysis suggests

The analysis points toward Conservation First as the strongest scenario across the criteria the partnership has stated. It survives most often (63 percent), achieves full partnership objectives most often (47 percent), produces the largest realized tax benefit in absolute dollars, and delivers the largest Foundation. The structural finding from Section 5.6, that the team's strongest defense gets stronger at smaller scale, is now quantified: each step down in lot count and up in Foundation contribution per lot improves both survival and objective achievement.

The analysis also surfaces that even Conservation, the strongest scenario, requires structural protections to materially improve its odds. Two refinements would move Conservation from 47 percent objective achievement toward a more partnership-acceptable band:

- **Closing-condition protections on entitlement timeline.** Roughly 14 percent of Conservation iterations fail debt retirement and dues coverage simultaneously, predominantly because of long entitlement delay (Section 6.3). Requiring entitlement clearance as a closing condition, the way Plan With Rigor handles POF determination and tax counsel opinion, removes that failure mode from the iterations the equity bears. The procedural template already exists in Plan With Rigor; Conservation would adopt it.

- **Foundation yield floor or more conservative target sizing.** Roughly 50 percent of Conservation iterations fall short of the $80M Foundation objective bar because realized yield comes in below the 6 percent historical mean. Two structural options exist: a Foundation yield floor mechanism (some structure under which the deal underwrites top-ups if realized yield underperforms a threshold; the specific structure would need to be designed), or sizing the per-lot Foundation contribution to land deterministically above $80M with margin, which would require a per-lot contribution somewhat higher than the current $3.2M.

The partnership decision is now better grounded than it was before this analysis. It is no longer "which of four roughly-defensible scenarios does the partnership prefer." It is whether to commit to Conservation First (with the refinements above), or to commit to a different scenario knowing what the analysis shows about its tradeoffs. Plan As Proposed makes the deal but accepts a 0.74 percent survival rate and zero objective achievement under stochastic variance. Plan With Rigor adds procedural protection but does not change the fundamentals: 12 percent survival, zero objective. Reduced Footprint produces high investor returns, with median MOIC above 5.0x, but stops short of regional-scale Foundation delivery (3.25 percent objective achievement, gated by the $80M Foundation bar).

The 47 percent finding for Conservation should not be read as endorsement of the deal as currently sized. About half the time, on the partnership's stated terms, the deal works. About half the time, it does not. That is the right number to plan around. The structural protections above are how the partnership would move Conservation toward a better number, not a guarantee that they would.
